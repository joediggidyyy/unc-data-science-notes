<td colspan="8"><p>Algorithmic Bias Explained:</p>
<p>How Automated Decision-Making Becomes Automated Discrimination</p></td>
<td style="text-align: right;"></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>What this is</td>
<td colspan="8"><ul>
<li><p>this <strong>report</strong> provides a <strong>baseline</strong> for <strong>advocates</strong> and <strong>policymakers</strong> on what <strong>algorithmic</strong> <strong>bias</strong> is and how it affects <strong>socioeconomic</strong> <strong>opportunity</strong> across sectors</p></li>
<li><p>outline <strong>real</strong>-<strong>world</strong> domains where <strong>algorithms</strong> act as a <strong>gatekeeper</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><p><strong>credit</strong> decisions</p></li>
<li><p><strong>hiring</strong> decisions</p></li>
<li><p><strong>government services</strong> (etc.)</p></li>
</ul></li>
</ul></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Algorithm (contextually bounded)</td>
<td colspan="8"><ul>
<li><p>a <strong>computer</strong>-<strong>based</strong> automated <strong>decision</strong> system</p></li>
<li><p>use statistical <strong>patterns</strong> and data <strong>analytics</strong> to make impactful <strong>decisions</strong></p></li>
<li><p><strong>how</strong> they <strong>work</strong> (basically)</p>
<ul>
<li><p>use <strong>attributes</strong> of a person (ex: <strong>income</strong>, <strong>age</strong>, etc.)</p></li>
<li><p><strong>output</strong> a <strong>prediction</strong></p></li>
</ul></li>
<li><p>prediction <strong>triggers</strong> <strong>action</strong></p></li>
</ul></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Algorithmic Bias</td>
<td colspan="8"><ul>
<li><p>algorithms are permitted to make decisions that produce unfair outcomes by arbitrarily elevating some demographics over others</p></li>
</ul></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Why This Matters</td>
<td colspan="8"><ul>
<li><p>these systems allocate access to opportunity and therefore can widen inequities</p></li>
</ul></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Origins of Algorithmic Bias</td>
<td colspan="8"><ul>
<li><p>training data encodes historical discrimination</p></li>
<li><p>a model can learn and reproduce those patterns</p></li>
<li><p>racial bias has been observed despite not specifying “race” in the training data</p></li>
</ul></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Affected Domains</td>
<td colspan="8"><ul>
<li><p>health care</p>
<ul>
<li><p>algorithmic processing of health-related data can help reduce cost and improve diagnosis</p></li>
<li><p>bias can be asserted when tools are misused or deployed with proper training and governance</p></li>
</ul></li>
<li><p>employment</p>
<ul>
<li><p>non-representative datasets and structural bias can skew automated hiring</p></li>
</ul></li>
<li><p>housing</p>
<ul>
<li><p>risk-scoring and related tools can repackage entrenched links with race, devaluation, violence, and disinvestment into seemingly neutral risk metrics</p></li>
</ul></li>
<li><p>dynamic pricing</p>
<ul>
<li><p>major firms are known to employ “different people pay different prices” policies</p></li>
</ul></li>
</ul></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Legal Framing</td>

<li><p>there is no single general U.S. law that specifically prohibits algorithmic bias</p></li>
<li><p>anti-discrimination laws may apply depending on domain</p></li>
</ul></td>

</tr>

<tbody>
<tr>
<td colspan="2">History</td>
<td colspan="2"><ul>
<li><p>the word “<strong>technology</strong>” did <strong>not</strong> come into <strong>widespread</strong> <strong>use</strong> until the <strong>1930’s</strong></p></li>
<li><p>the term was <strong>employed</strong> as a <strong>distinction</strong> between “<strong>machinery</strong>” and “<strong>technology</strong>”</p></li>
<li><p>this was <strong>intended</strong> as a <strong>class</strong> <strong>distinction</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><p><strong>technology</strong>: high-class, <strong>professional</strong></p></li>
<li><p><strong>machinery</strong>: low-class, <strong>blue</strong>-<strong>collar</strong></p></li>
</ul></li>
</ul></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td colspan="2">Reification</td>
<td colspan="2"><ul>
<li><p>when an <strong>artifact</strong> gains its own “<strong>identity</strong>” distinct from the <strong>human</strong> that <strong>designed it</strong></p></li>
</ul></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td colspan="2">Computers</td>
<td colspan="2"><ul>
<li><p>in the 1970s, “<strong>computer</strong>” was equivalent to “<strong>woman</strong>”</p></li>
<li><p>most tasks that <strong>required</strong> <strong>calculation</strong> were handled by <strong>human</strong> <strong>computers</strong></p></li>
<li><p>these <strong>O.G. computers</strong> were conventionally <strong>women</strong> who were trained in <strong>mathematics</strong> or related fields</p></li>
<li><p>these <strong>women</strong> were <strong>treated</strong> as temporary <strong>unskilled</strong> workers <strong>without</strong> access to <strong>benefits</strong> or retirement</p></li>
<li><p>a <strong>man</strong> in a similar <strong>position</strong> would be labeled an “<strong>engineer</strong>” which is generally <strong>full</strong>-<strong>time</strong>, promotable, and came with <strong>retirement</strong> <strong>benefits</strong></p></li>
</ul></td>
</tr>
<tr>

</ul></td>

<td colspan="4">Can Algorithms be Neutral</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>What is an algorithm</td>
<td colspan="3"><ul>
<li><p>a set of <strong>rules</strong> or <strong>instructions</strong> used to carry out <strong>tasks</strong></p></li>
<li><p>can save <strong>time</strong>, conserve <strong>resources</strong>, and light the path to <strong>innovation</strong></p></li>
<li><p>for an algorithm to be <strong>useful</strong>, it must <strong>ingest</strong> training <strong>data</strong></p></li>
<li><p>this <strong>data</strong> is generally <strong>biased</strong> on <strong>human</strong> past <strong>experiences</strong> and <strong>decisions</strong></p></li>
<li><p>those <strong>affected</strong> by the embedded <strong>biases</strong> often are <strong>not</strong> <strong>aware</strong> of how or <strong>why</strong> certain <strong>decisions</strong> were made</p></li>
<li><p>algorithms <strong>learn</strong> the “<strong>rules</strong>” from the <strong>patterns</strong> revealed in the training <strong>data</strong> by these <strong>embedded</strong> human <strong>experiences</strong></p></li>
<li><p><strong>understanding</strong> these imbedded <strong>biases</strong> can guide enlightened <strong>training</strong> practices, especially in <strong>finance</strong>, <strong>health</strong>, <strong>education</strong>, and <strong>employment</strong> sectors</p></li>
<li><p>algorithmic <strong>transparency</strong> is <strong>not</strong> always <strong>helpful</strong>, especially when presented to <strong>non</strong>-data <strong>scientists</strong></p></li>
<li><p><strong>regulating</strong> algorithms is facilitated by <strong>access</strong> to and <strong>understanding</strong> of <strong>three</strong> <strong>things</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><p>the <strong>decision</strong>-making <strong>process</strong></p></li>
<li><p><strong>what</strong> led to the <strong>need</strong> and <strong>creation</strong> for the <strong>algorithm</strong></p></li>
<li><p>what <strong>training</strong> <strong>data</strong> was used to teach <strong>decision</strong>-<strong>making</strong> skills to the <strong>model</strong></p></li>
</ul></li>
</ul></td>

<td colspan="4" style="text-align: center;"><p>The Automation of Trust</p>
<p>In discussions over the blitzkrieg advancement of machine learning models, you hear it often said that we are on the precipice of an unprecedented shift in all facets of the cultural paradigm. I, for one, (welcome our…no, not that) cannot stop myself from jumping on the soapbox at any hint of a sympathetic ear. So much so, that even I am tired of hearing myself talk about it. In increasingly-frequent discussions, I find that, as with all polarizing issues, people generally fit into two generalized categories. 1) AI is going to save humanity and deliver the Star Trek future, and 2) AI is going to take all our jobs then raise up and destroy us. Both views are inherently flawed but bring to the forefront the issue of trust and raise questions about how this abstract and tenuous human construct will guide the development and utilization of developing technologies.</p>
<p>To contemplate the role of trust in adopting intelligent systems, one must first define trust. Trust can be deconstructed into three facets: dispositional, situational, and learned trust (Hoff &amp; Bashir, 2014). Dispositional trust varies by individual and sets a baseline from which human experiences can build, or break down, trust barriers. Situational trust is the most dynamic of the three, and can be affected by mood, short-term or recent impactful events, and an incalculable number of decision-directing parameters. The facet that is the most dangerous is learned trust, and probably not for the reasons that you are thinking.</p>
<p>The human brain is an incredible machine for pattern matching. It can be argued that pattern recognition is its only building block, upon which all other cognition is built, with learned trust being no exception. As intervention in our lives from AI models normalize, and agentic A.I. takes on more and more of the decision-making processes in our daily lives, the patterns for a dangerously unearned trust-bubble are setting in. Large parameter learning models are, at first, amazingly intuitive, but the more you work with them, the drawbacks and behavioral inconsistencies, potentially occluded from casual users, become glaring. This fact is not prominent in the many advertisements attempting to draw us into an agentic Coke vs Pepsi standoff. The industry narrative that consistently over-sells and over-promises is another factor inflating the A.I. trust-bubble.</p>
<p>The benefits of adopting the intelligent systems freely offered and aggressively marketed are difficult to ignore, and for the believers, the possibility of the Star Trek future lend to over-trust and rapid-adoption of this still-developing technology, The rapidity of adoption into military, government, and infrastructure is an insanely blind-eye-forward move that is both disturbing and a subject in need of stand-alone analysis beyond the scope of this article. The trust-bubble grows.</p>
<p>I am a believer, but I advise caution moving forward. We must not continue to grant unearned trust to systems we barely understand, or that inflated confidence threatens to pop in our faces.</p>
